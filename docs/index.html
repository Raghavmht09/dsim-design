SECTION 01

DSIM Reviewer Documentation

For stakeholders reviewing the DSIM wireframe prototype v2 (an early visual mockup of the product) with zero prior context on Lactalis, digital-shelf vocabulary, Bayesian MMM methodology, or the 5-stage customer journey. Read alongside with actual design prototype.

📊
What this is
A 7-page interactive dashboard prototype for DSIM (Digital Shelf Impact Measurement) — DataWeave's Bayesian Marketing Mix Model product for CPG brands selling on Walmart.com. It quantifies how 6 digital-shelf pillars drive sales, sizes the upside from moving each lever, and tracks whether shipped plays delivered the predicted lift. v2 adds a Marketing & Sales context page, Goal Builder entry on the Scenario Builder, and a team-shared Decision Log.
👤
Who the customer is
VP or Director of eCommerce / Revenue Growth Management at a $100M+ Walmart.com CPG brand. They own the Walmart P&L, allocate Walmart Connect spend, and answer to a CMO and CFO. Stats-literate, not statisticians. They want decisions, not models.
🎯
Lactalis case study
Reference case used throughout the prototype:
  • Model fit: R² = 0.91, MAPE = 5.9%
  • 17 Walmart stores in 4 mega-clusters
  • Headline: $30K/day reallocation upside ($10.9M/yr, Onsite Search → Offsite Display) at 90% CI
  • 3 sub-brands: Adult (52%), Baby (28%), Kids (20%)
  • Cross-sub-brand halo: Kids/Baby media → +12% incremental Adult sales

SECTION 02 · GLOSSARY

Pillars

DSIM tracks 6 pillars using customer-friendly names that map to underlying KPIs. Throughout the product, the customer-facing name appears first; the technical KPI appears in tooltips and footnotes. Competition is a headwind measured in decomposition, not a scored pillar.

Pillar Customer-facing Underlying KPI Why it matters Lactalis example
Availability
 Always Available On-Shelf Availability (OSA (On-Shelf Availability — whether your product is actually in stock and visible on the retailer's website)) — share of expected facings actually stocked Out-of-stock sessions are lost sessions. Even with perfect price and content, a sold-out PDP earns $0. Q3 OSA dipped to 78% during peak campaign → cost ~$2M lift
Content Quality
 Looking Great Content Quality Score (CQS (Content Quality Score — how complete and accurate your product listing is)) — weighted index of title, bullets, images, A+ coverage Walmart's search algorithm rewards complete content. Conversion lifts with image count and A+ presence. CQS = 7.2/10 across 142 SKUs; Q3 content fix landed 12 of 50 SKUs in window (capacity constraint)
Pricing
 Right Price Price index vs comp set; rollback presence; price elasticity (how much sales volume changes when price changes — e.g. −2.0 means a 1% price rise cuts sales by 2%) Walmart shoppers price-compare in basket. Out-of-band pricing kills add-to-cart. Southern Staples cluster facing private-label pressure on Adult cheese SKUs
Retail Media
 Promoted Walmart Connect spend, ROAS (Return on Ad Spend — how many dollars of sales you get back per dollar spent on ads), iROI (Incremental Return on Investment — additional revenue gained from an extra dollar of spend) per channel (Onsite Search, Offsite Display, Onsite Display, Sponsored Visibility) Paid placement is one of the largest controllable levers. DSIM's job is to keep ROAS above saturation. Onsite Search at 0.83× iROI (saturated); Offsite Display at 1.55× iROI (headroom) → $30K/day reallocation upside
Share of Search
 Easy To Find Share of Search (SoS (Share of Search — what percentage of relevant searches surface your product)) — % of category search impressions captured If shoppers can't find the SKU in search results, no other lever matters. SoS is the upstream funnel. SoS = 24.1%, down 0.5 pts WoW (Week-over-Week — comparing this week to last week); each +1 pt SoS ≈ +$45K quarterly sales
Reviews
 Reviews Star rating, review count, review velocity, review sentiment score Walmart's algorithm surfaces high-reviewed products; conversion rates correlate with rating. Poor reviews suppress media ROI — spend can't compensate for a 2-star product. 4.3★ avg across tracked SKUs; Localized Stores cluster below 4.0★ flagged as watch
Competition
 Competition headwind Competitor share of voice / spend / pricing pressure (treated as headwind in decomposition — not a pillar you improve, a force you account for) Sales drops can be your fault or theirs. Decomposition separates the two — Competition is shown as a negative bar, not a scored pillar. Headwind contributed −5% in Q2 decomposition, concentrated in Southern Staples

SECTION 03 · GLOSSARY

DSIM Methodology

Plain English definitions for every modeling term used in the prototype. Use the alphabet jump strip to scan, or the search bar above to filter.

TermPlain English
Bayesian MMMMarketing Mix Model that uses Bayesian inference — produces posterior distributions (confidence ranges) instead of single point estimates. Output: "$30K/day lift at 90% CI [$24K – $36K]."
RobynAn open-source marketing mix modeling library built by Meta. Uses Bayesian MMM that DSIM is built on. Industry-credible reference.
MAPEMean Absolute Percentage Error — average week-over-week prediction error. Lactalis = 5.9%. Below 10% is considered industry-grade.
R²Coefficient of determination — fraction of sales variance the model explains. Lactalis = 0.91, meaning 91% of weekly sales movement is explained by the model's inputs.
HoldoutA separate slice of data kept away from model training. Used to check that the model's accuracy is genuine — not just memorised patterns.
Response CurveThe shape of the relationship between a driver (e.g. spend) and sales. Almost always S-shaped (Hill function).
Hill functionMathematical form for a saturating curve. Produces a saturating curve with a flat start, steep growth zone, and diminishing returns at high input.
Flat zoneLow end of the response curve where adding more driver yields ~zero lift. Don't invest here.
Growth zoneSteep middle of the curve where each dollar of driver yields meaningful lift. Invest here.
Saturation zoneHigh end of the curve where adding more driver yields diminishing returns. Stop investing here.
ElasticityThe percentage sales change per percentage driver change at a given point. >1 = elastic, <1 = inelastic. Reported "at average" and "at optimal."
iROIIncremental Return on Investment — effect share ÷ investment share. iROI = 1.0 means the channel returns proportionally to its spend; >1.0 = underinvested; <1.0 = overinvested / saturated.
iROASIncremental Return on Ad Spend — incremental revenue ÷ ad spend. The dollar-denominated cousin of iROI.
Effect ShareThe fraction of total sales attributable to a driver (e.g., Promoted contributed 23% of Q2 sales).
Investment ShareThe fraction of total controllable spend allocated to a driver.
90% Confidence Interval (CI)The range within which the true value falls 90% of the time according to the Bayesian posterior. DSIM uses 90% CI throughout (v2 fix — v1.1 corrected the v1 bug where the Overview strip showed 95%). All CI references in v2 should read 90%.
Store ClusteringGrouping Walmart stores by sales pattern similarity. Lactalis = 17 stores in 4 mega-clusters: Western High Performers, Northern Growth Engines, Southern Staples, Localized Stores.
Halo EffectCross-product or cross-sub-brand sales lift driven by media or activity on a different SKU/sub-brand. Lactalis Kids/Baby media → +12% Adult sales.
Sub-brand modelingDecomposing sales separately by sub-brand to isolate sub-brand-specific drivers and cross-sub-brand halos.
BaselineThe sales the model predicts would have happened with zero marketing activity (organic demand). Lactalis Q2 baseline = 49% of total sales.
DecompositionBreaking total sales into baseline + each driver's contribution. Output: a waterfall chart that sums to total.
ReallocationMoving spend from a saturated channel to one in the growth zone, holding total spend constant. The headline lever DSIM produces.
No matching terms.

SECTION 05 · GLOSSARY

Lever Taxonomy (Scenario Builder)

The Scenario Builder (Page 5) groups levers into three classes. Each class has a colored left border on the section card. In v2, before seeing any lever sliders, users first select a Goal via the Goal Builder entry step.

Goal Builder entry step (v2 new)
Before exposing the 3 lever classes, users select a goal: Maximize Revenue · Protect Margin · Improve Share of Voice · Improve a Pillar · Custom. The selected goal contextually surfaces only the relevant lever subset — e.g. "Improve a Pillar" first asks which pillar, then shows only levers that influence that pillar. Pick Custom to see all 3 classes simultaneously. This reduces cognitive load for the most common use cases and makes the "right" lever set discoverable without scrolling through 13 sliders.
Direct

Direct controls

Plain English
Levers Lactalis can act on this week without anyone else's approval.
Who owns it
The eCommerce team / RGM team using DSIM.
Examples
Set Onsite Search spend; set Offsite Display spend; set rollback %; set content score target.
Influenced

Influenced targets

Plain English
Outcomes another team owns. Lactalis sets the goal, then briefs the partner team to execute.
Who owns it
Adjacent functions: Sales (for price), Ops (for availability), SEO/Merchandising (for SoS rank), Brand (for assortment expansion).
Examples
Push to list price; lift availability target; improve SoS rank; expand assortment.
External

External sensitivities

Plain English
Things Lactalis cannot control. Used to test how robust the plan is to outside conditions.
Who owns it
Nobody — they are the market.
Examples
Competitor sponsored spend; category seasonality index; Walmart traffic shift.
Why this matters: the same dashboard produces three different kinds of artifacts depending on which class you move:
  • Move only Direct → produces a plan you can ship Monday.
  • Move Direct + Influenced → produces a plan + a brief for the partner team.
  • Move External → produces a sensitivity / what-if for the leadership review.

The "Commitment summary" in the prediction panel counts moves in each class to make this triage visible.
